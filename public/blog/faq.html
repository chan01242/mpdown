    2. What is audio bitrate?

    The amount of bits (audio data) handled in a specific amount of time is known as the audio bitrate. It is commonly used to specify a file's audio quality and is expressed in kilobits per second (kbps).
    Superior audio quality is usually associated with a larger bitrate. But it also results in larger-sized files and higher bandwidth requirements. The technological process of converting sounds and images into data so they can be played on digital devices involves a lot of work behind the scenes. For instance, high-end streaming services usually employ a bitrate of 128 kbps (kilobits per second). Even with weaker internet connections, it guarantees a smooth streaming experience and offers respectable audio quality while consuming little bandwidth.
    A smaller bitrate, on the other hand, indicates that we are representing the audio with fewer data, which will result in a higher information loss and lower audio quality.
    How come? because comparable frequency ranges, subtleties, and other audio characteristics cannot be captured by a smaller bitrate.
    As a result, a reduced bitrate may result in audible distortions and artifacts; data and frequency loss during compression will cause a decrease in clarity.
    The ideal bitrate for audio depends on the particular use case, the kind of audio, and the network setup of your audience. For example, you may require a higher bitrate to produce audio for high-end systems. However, because internet speeds might vary, a smaller bitrate is more suited for streaming content online.
